Two Kinds of System Settings. Only One Travels.

When you configure any software tool, there are two kinds of settings. The first kind is platform-specific: it describes how the tool behaves, and it only works inside that tool. If you export it, it means nothing anywhere else. The second kind is identity-level: it describes something true about you, independent of any specific tool. It works anywhere the system accepts it.

Most people treat their AI configuration as the first kind. They think of it as “my Claude settings” or “my ChatGPT setup” — something tied to the platform, something that needs to be rebuilt from scratch when things change.

What you’ve built in this course is the second kind. The operator profile describes how you process information, how you make decisions, what your failure modes are. Those things are true whether you’re talking to Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or an AI that doesn’t exist yet. The behavioral contract describes the posture you need from any AI you work with seriously. The gears describe the phases of work your specific workflow requires. The ritual describes how sessions should open and close to compound over time. None of this is Claude-specific. None of it is tied to any platform.

This is what it means to build an identity layer instead of a tool configuration. The distinction isn’t about how the text is written — it’s about what the text describes. Tool configurations describe the tool. Identity layers describe the operator. You’ve been writing about yourself, not about the software. That’s why it travels.

TOOL CONFIGURATION

The Portability Audit

Most people don’t know exactly what’s in their system prompt. They added things over time — some from this course, some from prompts they found online, some constraints they wrote to fix specific behaviors that annoyed them. Some of that content is identity-level and will travel perfectly. Some of it is a workaround for a specific model behavior and will be irrelevant on a different platform or model version.

The portability audit takes ten minutes. It gives you a clean separation: here’s what I protect, here’s what I adapt. You only need to run it before a major transition — a platform switch, a significant model upgrade, a role change. The rest of the time, your system runs without maintenance.

The audit has two parts. A quick guided frame for what you’re looking for, then four questions you answer by reading through your own system prompt.

THE FRAME

WHAT TO LOOK FOR

01
Yours — content that describes you
Profile answers. Behavioral constraints you identified from your own patterns. Gear definitions based on how your work actually flows. Session ritual structure. If you deleted the platform and ran this on a blank AI, it would still make sense — because it’s about you, not the tool.
02
Platform-specific — content that compensates for tool behavior
Instructions like “don’t use markdown unless asked” written because this model over-formatted. Constraints like “don’t repeat my question back to me” written because this version had a specific tendency. These are workarounds, not identity. They may not be needed on a different tool or model.

THE AUDIT

FOUR QUESTIONS — RUN THESE ON YOUR SYSTEM PROMPT NOW

DIAGNOSTIC — OPEN YOUR SYSTEM PROMPT AND ANSWER THESE
Q1 — What describes me vs. what describes the tool?

Go line by line. For each instruction, ask: if I pasted this into a completely different AI with no prior context, would it still make sense? If yes — it’s yours. If it reads as a workaround for something tool-specific — flag it for review on the next transition.

Q2 — Which constraints did I write to fix annoying behavior?

These are the most likely to be platform-specific. “Don’t use bullet points for everything” may be essential on one model and irrelevant on another. Flag any constraint that was written reactively in response to a specific model tendency rather than something true about how you work.

Q3 — What’s in here that I don’t remember adding?

Every system prompt has content that drifted in over time and no longer reflects anything intentional. This is dead weight. It doesn’t break portability, but it dilutes the signal. Identify it for removal regardless of whether you’re switching platforms.

Q4 — If I had to reinstall this on a new tool in five minutes, what are the five most important things?

This surfaces the load-bearing content. The five things you name are almost certainly your identity layer. Everything else is configuration that can be adapted or dropped without losing the system.

A New Model Ships. What Do You Do?

This happens regularly now. A new model arrives that’s meaningfully better than the one you’ve been using — better reasoning, longer context, more nuanced instruction-following. And you have two instincts, both wrong.

The first instinct is to start fresh. Open the new model, begin a new conversation, don’t carry over the old setup because you want the clean experience unencumbered by old constraints. This feels right because it feels efficient. It’s not. You’re leaving months of accumulated calibration on the table — every constraint you wrote that compensated for a real pattern in how you work, every gear that reflects an actual phase transition in your workflow, every profile answer that took sessions of friction to get right. Gone. You start from zero on a better model, which is often worse than starting from a calibrated position on the previous one.

The second instinct is to copy everything over unchanged. Same system prompt, new model. This is closer to right, but it has a failure mode: some of what you wrote was compensating for specific behaviors of the old model that the new one doesn’t have. Constraints that were load-bearing on the old model become dead weight on the new one — or worse, they constrain behavior that the new model would have gotten right without them. You keep the calibration but lose the new capabilities. Not a rebuild, but not an upgrade either.

The right move is neither. It’s diff and merge.

THE THREE OPTIONS

01
Start fresh — the default move
Open the new model with no context. Cost: you lose months of accumulated calibration. The new model is better hardware running a worse configuration than your old one.
02
Copy everything unchanged — the wrong move
Same system prompt, new model. Cost: constraints written to compensate for old behavior become dead weight or actively constrain new capabilities. You preserve calibration but forfeit the upgrade.
03
Diff and merge — the right move
Preserve what’s load-bearing. Update what’s outdated. Add what the new model makes newly possible. This is the next tab.

Diff and Merge Preserves What’s Load-Bearing. Overwrite Loses It.

The calibration you’ve built is the most valuable thing in your system. It took time, friction, and real work to accumulate. The upgrade process should protect it — not treat it as disposable in the name of a fresh start.

Not a Rebuild. An Upgrade.

Diff and merge is a concept from software development: when two versions of a codebase diverge, you don’t pick one and throw the other away. You compare them systematically, keep what’s still valid, update what’s changed, and integrate what’s new. The same logic applies to your AI OS when a significant model upgrade ships.

The process has four steps. Each one is a specific question you answer by running your existing system prompt against the new model. The whole process takes under an hour the first time, and gets faster as you understand what’s genuinely load-bearing in your system vs. what was always a workaround.

THE PROCESS

THE FOUR-STEP DIFF AND MERGE

01
Run the old profile on the new model
Load your current system prompt into the new model unchanged. Have a normal working session. Note what changed in output quality — what got better, what got worse, what’s different in unexpected ways. This is your baseline for the merge.
02
Identify legacy constraints
Go through your behavioral contract and gear definitions. For each constraint, ask: was this written to compensate for a specific old behavior? If the new model doesn’t exhibit that behavior — the constraint is legacy. Flag it for removal or simplification. Don’t remove it yet; just flag.
03
Run the Interview Gear on the new model
Use the Interview Gear from Unit 4 to generate a fresh profile on the new model. Compare what it produces to your existing profile. Where they diverge, investigate: has something changed in how you work, or is the new model just reading you differently? This surfaces gaps in the existing profile that the upgrade is a good moment to fix.
04
Merge the results
Update what’s outdated (the legacy constraints you flagged). Keep what’s load-bearing (the profile content, the gear architecture, the ritual structure). Add what the new model makes newly possible — capabilities or behaviors the old model couldn’t reliably deliver that you can now build into the contract or gears.
How do I know if a constraint is legacy vs. still load-bearing?
Run a session without it. If the new model produces the behavior the constraint was preventing — keep the constraint. If it doesn’t produce that behavior without being told — the constraint is legacy. Remove it. The new model’s defaults have already solved what you were compensating for.
What if the new model behaves so differently that nothing seems to apply?
This is rare, but it happens when a model generation is a significant jump. In this case, treat it as a BOOT from scratch — but use your existing profile as the reference document, not a blank slate. Interview Gear first, compare to existing profile, keep what’s still accurate, rebuild only what has genuinely changed.
How often should I do this?
When output quality degrades noticeably, or when a significant model version ships. Not every minor update. The system is designed to run without maintenance — the diff and merge is for meaningful upgrades, not routine operation.

What the System Becomes Over Time

There’s an argument for this system that has nothing to do with portability, and everything to do with time. It’s the compounding argument — and it’s the reason the system you’ve built is genuinely difficult for anyone else to replicate, regardless of what tools they use or what models they have access to.

Compounding, in the financial sense, means that gains build on previous gains rather than starting from zero each time. The curve bends upward not because the rate of return changes, but because the base keeps growing. The same dynamic applies here — but what compounds isn’t money. It’s calibration.

On day one, your system works. You have a baseline profile, a basic contract, a couple of gears. The AI understands you well enough to be useful. That’s already more than most people have.

By month one, something has shifted. The profile has been refined through sessions where it was wrong and you corrected it. The contract has clauses that were written after you noticed yourself falling into a specific failure mode. The gears have been tuned to the actual rhythm of your work, not just the theoretical version of it. The state document has history. The AI isn’t just responding to what you say — it’s operating from an increasingly accurate model of how you work.

By month six, the gap between this system and a fresh start is large. Not because the AI changed. Because you built something on top of it that took six months of real work to accumulate. Every session that closed deliberately added to it. Every constraint you refined sharpened it. Every gear you built or adjusted made it more specific to your actual workflow. The system is now shaped around you in a way that no one else can replicate by copying your system prompt — because your system prompt is the output of six months of calibration, not the input to it.

WHY THIS IS A MOAT

The word “moat” in business means a durable competitive advantage that’s hard for others to replicate. Most AI moats people talk about are fragile — model access, prompt libraries, fancy workflows. These are replicable. Anyone with access to the same model and a good prompt engineer can get close.

Accumulated operator context is not replicable. Someone could copy your system prompt today and get a reasonable approximation of your setup. They couldn’t copy the six months of calibration underneath it. They couldn’t copy the specific ways your profile has been refined, the constraints you identified from your own failure modes, the gears that were built around your actual work phases. The document is copyable. The relationship that produced it isn’t.

This Is the Moat. Not the AI’s Capabilities. The Operator’s Accumulated Context.

No one can buy this. No one can copy it. It grows with use. Every session compounds it. Every refinement deepens it. The system you have in six months will be qualitatively different from the one you have today — not because the AI changed, but because you kept working with it deliberately.
