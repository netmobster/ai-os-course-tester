THE WRONG DIAGNOSIS

The Wrong Diagnosis

The entire AI productivity industry is organized around a false premise: that the problem is how you phrase your request. That there's a technique you haven't learned, a template you haven't found, a magic construction that unlocks better responses.

Prompt libraries exist because of this premise. “Prompt engineering” courses exist because of this premise. Every “10x your AI results” post on LinkedIn exists because of this premise.

It's wrong. The problem is upstream of the prompt entirely. And until you fix the upstream problem, better prompts are like speaking louder to someone who doesn't speak your language.

Here's what most people have already tried — and why none of it fixed anything:

WHAT YOU'VE TRIED
  • Better phrasing
  • Prompt libraries
  • Copying templates from Twitter / LinkedIn
  • Asking more specifically
  • Finding the “right” AI tool
None of these fix the upstream problem.

THE REFRAME

The AI Doesn't Know Who You Are

The AI is giving you generic answers because it has no idea who you are.

Every session starts from zero. You open a chat and the AI is meeting you for the first time. It doesn't know what you do, how you think, what a good output looks like for you, what your failure modes are, or what kind of response will land versus get dismissed. It's making its best guess from the words in front of it — and those words don't contain a model of you.

You're not prompting badly. You're asking a stranger.

The gap between what you're doing and what you actually need:

WHAT YOU'RE DOING
Optimizing the prompt
WHAT YOU ACTUALLY NEED
A persistent model of you
THE INSIGHT

“The AI is not broken. It's just meeting you for the first time. Every time.”

THE DISTINCTION

The Distinction That Changes Everything

Here's what most people do when they try to fix this: they tell the AI facts about themselves. Their role. Their preferences. Their format requirements.

Facts are almost useless. What the AI actually needs is your wiring — the structural features of how you operate under pressure. Not what you prefer, but how you function when it matters.

The difference is easiest to see side by side:

FACTS — surface dataWIRING — what the AI actually needs
“I work in marketing.”“Under pressure I default to doing before thinking. My first instinct is always to build the thing, and I often build the wrong thing.”
“I like bullet points.”“I reject answers that hedge. If the AI gives me options instead of a recommendation, I ignore it.”
“I'm a morning person.”“I spiral when there's no clear deliverable. Ambiguity turns into endless option-generating.”
THE DISTINCTION

“One is data. The other is a model. The AI has been getting the data. It needs the model.”

Notice the difference. Facts describe surface preferences. Wiring describes the structural patterns that determine whether a response is actually useful — the decision function, the failure mode, the trust signal.

This is worth pausing on before you continue.

EXERCISE — DO THIS BEFORE MOVING ON

What you're doing:

Converting three things you know about yourself into wiring statements.

Why it matters:

The quality of your self-profile is almost entirely determined by this distinction. A profile full of facts gives the AI data. A profile full of wiring gives it a model. The responses feel completely different.

The steps:

  1. Write down three things you'd normally put in a system prompt about yourself. (“I'm a morning person.” “I work in marketing.” “I like concise answers.”)
  2. For each one, ask: what does this actually mean for how I make decisions or what I need from a response?
  3. Rewrite each one as a structural statement about how you operate.
WEAK — still a fact
“I value clear answers.”
Tells the AI nothing about when or why.
STRONG — wiring
“If the AI gives me multiple options without recommending one, I treat the response as a failure — even if the options are good.”
Tells the AI exactly how to calibrate.

What done looks like:

Each rewritten statement should answer a specific failure mode or decision pattern — not a preference. “I need one recommendation, not a list” is wiring because it tells the AI what to do differently. “I like concise answers” is still a fact — it doesn't tell the AI when conciseness matters or what makes a response feel too long for you.

If you can read your rewritten statement and imagine the AI giving you a noticeably different response — you've found the wiring.

THE RECOGNITION MOMENT

The Question That Breaks The Frame

“How does it know what a good output looks like for you?”

It doesn't. You've never told it.

Every response you've ever gotten from AI was built on a guess. A guess about your standards. A guess about your failure modes. A guess about what will land and what will get dismissed. The AI isn't bad at its job — it's been working without a brief.

THE SHIFT

THE ARTIFACT

Your 5-Sentence Self-Profile

Five sentences. One paste. That's the complete install for this unit.

Here's the format — and what each sentence is actually doing:

01
Sentence 1 — Role + Mandate
My role is [role]. My current mandate is [what you're actually trying to do right now].
Not your job title — the mission. “I'm three months into a new role and my mandate is to not break a $500K/month paid machine while slowly evolving it toward AI-first approaches.” The AI now knows what you're optimizing for.
02
Sentence 2 — Active Failure Mode
I fail most when [your most active failure mode right now].
Not a theoretical weakness — the specific thing that cost you in the last six months. “I fail most when I jump to building before I've validated the problem. My instinct is to ship first and assess the damage later.”
03
Sentence 3 — Spiral Trigger
I spiral when [the condition that sends you into unproductive loops].
“I spiral when there's no clear deliverable. Ambiguity is my kryptonite — I'll generate options forever if I don't know what done looks like.”
04
Sentence 4 — Output Standard
A useful output for me looks like [what actually makes a response actionable for you].
This is the sentence most people write poorly. “I like concise answers” is still a fact. “A useful output gives me one clear recommendation with the trade-off named — I can evaluate a recommendation, I can't evaluate a list” is wiring. Make it specific.
05
Sentence 5 — Trust Signal
I trust a response when [what makes you accept versus dismiss].
“I trust a response when it pushes back on something I said. Agreement makes me suspicious. Friction makes me trust.” Without this line, the AI defaults to validation — even when you need a challenge.
THE INSTALL

In Claude, go to Projects → New Project → paste the five sentences as the system prompt. In ChatGPT, use Custom Instructions. In Gemini, use System Instructions. The syntax varies. The architecture is the same everywhere.

YOUR TURN

What you're doing:

Writing your 5-sentence self-profile.

Why it matters:

This is the install. Everything else in this course builds on it. A rough profile that changes your responses is worth more than a polished one you never finish.

The steps:

  1. Open a blank document or note.
  2. Write one sentence for each of the five slots above: role + mandate, active failure mode, spiral trigger, output standard, trust signal.
  3. For each sentence, use the test: would the AI respond differently if it knew this? If the answer is no, it's still a fact. Go deeper on that sentence.
  4. Paste the finished five sentences into a Claude Project, ChatGPT Custom Instructions, or Gemini System Instructions.
  5. Ask a question you'd normally ask. Note what changed.

What done looks like:

A finished profile passes this test — read each sentence and ask: would the AI respond differently if it knew this? If the answer is yes for all five, the profile is working. If any sentence could apply to most people, it's still a fact. Go deeper on that sentence.

THE GROWTH EDGE

This Works. But It's Still Manual.

The five-sentence profile works. But it's still manual. You wrote it once. It reflects who you were when you wrote it. It doesn't evolve when your role changes, when a new failure mode surfaces, when your trust signal shifts.

There's also a deeper problem: writing a profile requires knowing your own wiring. But most people don't — not clearly. They produce facts in a wiring-shaped format and wonder why the responses don't change much.

What you actually need isn't a profile you write. It's a profile that gets excavated — question by question, until the structural features surface on their own.

COMMON QUESTIONS

Four Questions This Unit Generates

Q: Does this work on ChatGPT?

Yes. Use Custom Instructions or a GPT system prompt. The profile format is platform-agnostic. The delivery mechanism changes; the artifact doesn't.

Q: How often do I update the profile?

When something significant changes — a new role, a new failure mode, a new standard for output. Not after every session. Think quarterly, not daily.

Q: Is this the same as a custom instruction?

Custom instructions are the feature. The 5-sentence profile is what you put inside it. Most people use custom instructions for preferences. This uses them for wiring.

Q: What if I don't know what my wiring is?

Write the profile anyway. The format is designed to surface what you don't know yet. The act of writing it is part of the install.
